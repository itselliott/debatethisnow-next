A virtual debate game is the easiest way to keep a classroom or club engaged across a screen — every student gets to argue, the structure forces them to listen, and a scoring system gives everyone a result to point at. This guide walks through hosting one from scratch.

It's written for teachers, parents running a homeschool group, and debate-club coaches who want to do round-robin practice without a tournament.

## Before the session — pick your format

You have three sane choices:

**1. Pairs debate (recommended for groups of 4–20).** Students pair up, each pair gets a topic, they debate for 5–10 minutes, the rest of the class votes. Rotate pairs every round. Fast, high-engagement, light setup.

**2. Round-robin debate (best for advanced clubs).** Every student debates every other student over a session — 3 rounds each, Elo-style scoring. Heavier setup, but the leaderboard at the end keeps everyone trying.

**3. Spectator debate game (best for younger or shyer groups).** Two students debate at a time while the rest score and ask questions. Lower pressure on participants, still very engaging because the spectator role is genuinely fun if you let them ask follow-ups.

Pick one before the session starts and tell the students which format you're running. Hybrid formats invented mid-session collapse.

## Step-by-step run sheet

### Pairs debate (recommended starter format)

1. **Open the session with a 5-minute icebreaker.** Pick a fun topic — "cereal is a soup," "a hot dog is a sandwich" — and have the whole class shout "for" or "against" so everyone calibrates to the format.
2. **Announce the round.** Pair students. Give each pair a topic. Assign a side (don't let them pick — picking takes 5 minutes; assigning takes 5 seconds).
3. **Give them prep time.** 2 minutes is enough for ages 12+. For ages 8–11 give 5 minutes.
4. **Run the debate.** 3 rounds — opening (60 sec each), rebuttal (45 sec each), closing (30 sec each). Use a visible timer. Cut speakers off at the buzzer; this is the most important rule and the one teachers most often skip.
5. **Vote.** Spectators vote on the winner. With DebateThis, vote tallies show live during the closing round.
6. **30-second debrief.** Ask the loser what they'd do differently and the winner what worked. Skip the "good job everyone" wrap — it teaches nothing.
7. **Rotate. Next pair.**

### Round-robin format (advanced clubs)

Same per-round structure as pairs, but every student plays every other student over the session. Track wins, losses, and an Elo-style score. DebateThis does this automatically for ranked matches; for off-platform, a Google Sheet with one row per student and a running score column works.

### Spectator format (younger groups)

Same as pairs, but only one pair debates per round while the rest are spectators. Spectators get a worksheet: write down the strongest argument they heard, write down a question they'd ask each side, vote on the winner.

## Topic selection by age

- **Ages 8–12.** Everyday topics work best. "Pineapple belongs on pizza," "school should start later," "kids should have phones at school," "homework should be banned." Avoid politics unless the class has done prep.
- **Ages 13–15.** Mix everyday + lightweight policy. "Social media has done more harm than good," "voting should be mandatory," "AI art should be copyrightable," "school uniforms reduce inequality."
- **Ages 16–18.** Full policy and philosophy. "Universal basic income is inevitable," "free will is an illusion," "we owe future generations more than past generations owed us."
- **College and adult.** Anything from the [topic catalog](/topics) including the harder ethics and economics tracks.

## What goes wrong, and the fix

**Students dominate or freeze.** Use shorter speech times. 60-second openings stop both problems — dominating students can't grandstand, frozen students only have to fill a minute.

**Audience checks out.** Give spectators a job: a worksheet, a vote, a follow-up question they have to ask at the end.

**Coach becomes the bottleneck.** Switch to a platform with built-in judging or audience voting so the coach can move between rooms rather than judge every round.

**Same students always win.** Add a "swap sides" variant — same topic, same students, swap which side they argue. The reliable winner usually struggles when forced into their less-favored side, which equalizes the room quickly.

**Topics go stale.** Refresh from the [topics catalog](/topics) between sessions. Filter by category so you don't accidentally run "Universal basic income" three weeks in a row.

## Quick-start: 30-minute classroom session

If you want a copy-paste session plan:

- **Minutes 0–3.** Introduce the format. Pair students up. Assign topics + sides.
- **Minutes 3–5.** Prep time.
- **Minutes 5–25.** Five 4-minute debate rounds (4 pairs go, the rest spectate + vote).
- **Minutes 25–28.** Quick leaderboard share — who won, who had the strongest argument.
- **Minutes 28–30.** Pick the topic for next session as a class.

This works for a 4-pair class of 8 students. Scale up or down by adjusting pair count and round length.
